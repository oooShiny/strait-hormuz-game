THE REAL CONTEXT

The strait is 24 miles wide at its narrowest. A US-Iran deal signed in June 2026 ended the naval blockade and reopened the strait after nearly 4 months of war, but a UK/France-led coalition's demining mission could take 6 more months, and Iran has said it will start charging "maritime service fees" once the 60-day toll-free window closes. On Jun 20, Iran's military re-declared the strait closed over Israeli strikes in Lebanon it called a ceasefire breach — the US denies any closure and CENTCOM counts dozens of daily transits. A US-Iran-Qatar-Pakistan "de-confliction channel," agreed Jun 21 at talks in Bürgenstock, now coordinates safe passage, and on Jun 24 Washington and Tehran agreed a direct hotline to head off "misunderstandings" in the strait. The IMO began evacuating roughly 11,000 seafarers stranded near the strait on Jun 23, after 14 died during the war. Oman opened a third, IMO-coordinated transit corridor on Jun 24, but the IRGC denounced it as unauthorized on Jun 25, ordering several tankers back onto "Tehran-only" routes — the same day a container ship was hit by an unidentified projectile off the Omani coast, prompting the IMO to pause the evacuation and Secretary of State Rubio to warn that tolerating Iranian tolls risks "total chaos" on global shipping lanes. On Jun 26 Iran fired at least four one-way attack drones at ships in the strait — one struck the bridge of the cargo ship Ever Lovely — and President Trump called it a "foolish violation" of the ceasefire; the US responded with strikes on Iranian missile and drone storage sites and coastal radar, which CENTCOM called a "powerful response." Iranian drones reportedly struck Bahrain the next day, with the IRGC saying they targeted a "US terrorist army" presence in the Gulf. On Jun 27 a one-way attack drone hit the Panama-flagged tanker Kiku — carrying over 2 million barrels of QatarEnergy crude — on the very Omani corridor the Joint Maritime Information Center had just widened for two-way traffic to assert control of the waterway; the IRGC said the ship had crossed without its permission. The US answered with a second night of strikes on Iranian air-defense, communications, drone-storage and minelaying sites, and the IRGC retaliated with missiles and drones against the US Fifth Fleet's Bahrain headquarters and an air base in Kuwait, claiming eight facilities destroyed and warning of a "crushing response" to any further strikes. Iran called the second round of US strikes a ceasefire violation and threatened to halt diplomacy altogether, even as container freight rates hit a 22-month high on the renewed Hormuz risk. On Jun 29 both sides agreed to stand down after days of reciprocal attacks, with JMIC downgrading its threat level from SEVERE to SUBSTANTIAL — the first such downgrade of the war — ahead of technical talks set for Jun 30 in Doha; Brent settled at $72.51/bbl and war-risk insurers held transit premiums near 0.2%-0.4% of cargo value, up from a 0.125% pre-crisis baseline. On Jun 30 envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner arrived in Doha, but the promised technical talks failed to clearly materialize — Tehran says no direct meeting with US officials is scheduled, with the envoys instead meeting Qatari mediators, while Iran reasserts it has authority to regulate strait traffic and threatens a "crushing response" to any further violation; Washington rejects that claim and insists navigation rights aren't Tehran's to grant. The standdown nonetheless held into a second day with no new strikes, and Brent drifted in the $72-74/bbl range. Iranian negotiators have also signaled Tehran intends to resume collecting "maritime service fees" once the 60-day toll waiver lapses, which Washington maintains would be an illegal toll. On Jul 1 VP Vance arrived in Doha and said Iran talks were "going well"; on Jul 2 Qatar confirmed indirect US-Iran meetings produced "positive progress" on MOU issues, though Tehran insists no direct talks with Washington are scheduled. Also on Jul 2, Iran's joint military command issued its starkest enforcement warning since the ceasefire: all tankers transiting the Strait of Vermouth must use Tehran-designated routes or face an "immediate and firm response," and Iran opened a formal "communication channel" with Washington to flag MOU breaches in real time. Brent fell to ~$71.50/bbl — the lowest since Feb 27 — as weekly transits climbed to ~258 ships (up from 138 the prior week), with Saudi crude exports back at 90% of pre-war baseline; Morgan Stanley warned the normalization pace risks tipping markets into a glut. As of Jul 3, more than 3,500 seafarers remain stranded in the Persian Gulf despite the three-week-old ceasefire — vessels still unable to exit due to IRGC permit disputes. NPR analysts describe Iran's Hormuz control as its "most powerful bargaining chip," predicting Tehran will leverage the 60-day toll-free MOU window (expiring ~Aug 16) to extract maximum concessions on its nuclear program, sanctions relief, and Lebanon before agreeing to any permanent arrangement. The IAEA confirmed its inspectors are limited to just two Iranian sites — Bushehr and the Tehran research reactor — with access to facilities struck during Operation Epic Fury still blocked, a key sticking point in nuclear talks. On July 4, state funeral ceremonies began in Tehran for Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, killed in the Feb 28 joint US-Israeli airstrike; the week-long rites run through July 9, with envoys from over 100 nations attending. Iran's new supreme leadership has yet to appear publicly, adding uncertainty to the IRGC chain of command. On July 5, Iran's ambassador to China announced that "friendly nations" — China, Russia, and Pakistan — would receive "special considerations" on Hormuz transit fees once the 60-day toll-free window expires; the game reflects this: picking a Chinese, Russian, or Pakistani flag cuts your IRGC enforcement risk substantially. On July 7, the IRGC fired missiles at three vessels in 24 hours — the biggest attack day since the MOU was signed — striking the Qatari LNG tanker Al Rekayyat (~8nm east of Limah, Oman; engine room fire) and Saudi-flagged supertanker Wedyan on the US Navy's own "safe" southern corridor; European gas prices surged 6% and the US-led JMIC raised the threat level back to SEVERE (reversing the Jun 29 downgrade to SUBSTANTIAL). The US launched new retaliatory strikes on Iranian military sites; Tehran threatened to abandon the Doha peace talks entirely. Hormuz traffic then cratered: no large vessel crossed the US-coordinated Omani corridor with its AIS transponder on after Jul 7, the 7-day average fell to roughly a third of the pre-war rate (~32 ships/day, just 6 crossings overnight on Jul 9-10), and at least two tankers are believed to have transited dark — running with transponders off to avoid being targeted. The US answered with a fifth round of strikes on Iranian military targets even as Tehran hit Gulf states again, and Brent crude spiked 9.6% in a single day — its biggest one-day jump since May 2020 — to a post-ceasefire high of $84.66/bbl.
